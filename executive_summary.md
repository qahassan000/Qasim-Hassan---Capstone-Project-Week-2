# RetailGlobe Customer Segmentation
### Executive Summary — Prepared for the Board

---

## The Problem We Solved

For years, RetailGlobe has been sending the same emails and promotions to every customer — from someone who has spent thousands of pounds with us over dozens of orders, to someone who bought once two years ago and never came back. We hired a data analyst to go through a full year of our sales records and answer a simple question: are all our customers really the same? The answer is no. Using 124,000 transactions from 3,994 customers, we have now placed every customer into one of four clear groups — each with a different value to the business and a different strategy going forward. The four groups we identified are statistically well-defined, accounting for 85% of the variation in how our customers behave, which gives us confidence these are real and meaningful distinctions, not just guesswork.

---

## Top 3 Insights

1. **A tiny group of customers is keeping the lights on.** Just 147 customers — less than 4% of our base — are responsible for roughly 40% of our total revenue. These are people who buy from us regularly, have bought recently, and spend significantly. If we lost half of them tomorrow, we would feel it immediately.

2. **We have a volume problem internationally, not a quality problem.** Almost 88p of every £1 we earn comes from UK customers — but when we looked closely at how much individual customers spend per order, our French, German, and Dutch customers spend roughly the same as UK customers (around £250–£340 each). The UK dominates our revenue because we have thousands of UK customers and only dozens in each other country. This is an important finding: international customers are not worse customers — we simply do not have enough of them yet.

3. **One in four customers never came back.** Roughly 25% of our customers placed a single order and disappeared. We spent money acquiring them and got one transaction in return. There is significant revenue sitting in that group that a simple, targeted re-engagement campaign could recover.

---

## Our Four Customer Groups

| Group | Size | Share of Revenue | Who they are |
|-------|------|-----------------|--------------| 
| **Champions** | 147 customers | ~40% | Buy often, buy recently, spend the most |
| **Loyal Customers** | 876 customers | ~37% | Regular buyers, strong relationship |
| **At-Risk** | 2,440 customers | ~20% | Haven't bought in a while, spending is low |
| **Dormant** | 531 customers | ~3% | Have not engaged in many months |

---

## 3 Recommendations for Emma

**1. Protect the Champions — personally.**
Write to all 147 Champions this week. Not a bulk email — a personal message from Emma. Thank them by name, give them early access to new products, and assign them a direct contact at RetailGlobe. The cost of this is negligible. The cost of losing even ten of them is not.

**2. Win back At-Risk customers before Christmas.**
Our biggest sales month is October. We have 2,440 customers who have started to drift away. Send them a personalised offer — a 10–15% discount, valid for three weeks — in September, before the Christmas rush begins. Even a 5% response rate would generate tens of thousands of pounds in recovered revenue at exactly the right time of year.

**3. Fix the "one and done" problem — and use it to grow internationally.**
Every new customer who places their first order should automatically receive a follow-up email two weeks later — a thank you, a relevant product suggestion, and a small incentive to return. This addresses the 25% one-time buyer problem domestically. It is also the right starting point for international growth: since our data shows international customers spend just as much per order as UK customers, acquiring more of them and converting them to repeat buyers is a direct path to revenue growth that does not depend entirely on the UK market.

---

## What This Analysis Doesn't Tell Us

This analysis tells us *what* customers have done — it does not tell us *why*. We do not know why At-Risk customers stopped buying, whether it was price, a bad experience, or a competitor. We also cannot yet tell the difference between a wholesale business customer and an individual consumer in our data — these are very different buyers and may need separate strategies in future. The international spending finding is promising but comes with a caveat: we have far fewer international customers in our records, so the comparison is not perfectly equal. It points in an encouraging direction, but would benefit from a dedicated international analysis once we have more data.
